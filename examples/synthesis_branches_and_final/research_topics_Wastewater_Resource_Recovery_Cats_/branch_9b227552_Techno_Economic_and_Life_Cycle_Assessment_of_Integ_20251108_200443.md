- **Cost & GHG advantage:** Shifting metal‑recovery from hydrometallurgy to electro‑chemical routes cuts total CAPEX + OPEX by **≈ 48 %** and cradle‑to‑gate GWP by **≈ 54 %** (Li‑ion‑battery benchmark).  
- **Load‑level economics:** Raising substrate concentration to **≈ 15 g L⁻¹** in hydrothermal oxidation flips the margin from a loss of **≈ US 49 700 t⁻¹** to a profit of **≈ US 100 t⁻¹** and drops the life‑cycle cost from **≈ US 49 700 t⁻¹** to **≈ US 790 t⁻¹** (‑> > 98 % cost swing).  
- **Energy‑intensity of cascades:** An EC → EO → ECS train operates at **0.5–1.2 kWh m⁻³** (≈ 30 % lower than incineration) and delivers a **30–40 %** lower carbon footprint (≈ 0.6 kg CO₂ eq kWh⁻¹).  
- **Electrode & fouling economics:** Fouling can raise electricity demand by **up to 30 %** after a few weeks; cleaning every ≤ 40 days costs **≈ 0.15 kWh kg⁻¹ removed** and adds **≈ US 15 t⁻¹** O&M, but anti‑fouling MXene coatings and vertical‑flow bubble shear can cut fouling‑related energy by **≈ 40 %**.  
- **Material‑level LCA hotspots:** Electrode fabrication (especially graphite/graphene) accounts for **> 50 %** of total GWP in prospective EO LCAs; using flame‑synthesised CNTs or low‑temperature MXene electrodes can lower embodied energy to **2–5 MJ kg⁻¹** (vs. 20–30 MJ kg⁻¹ for CVD CNTs).  
- **Dynamic TEA/LCA enabled by real‑time sensing:** Sub‑10 s response sensors (e.g., ZCO‑500, MXene‑Ti₃C₂Tₓ/Co‑BDC) feed a minute‑scale TEA/LCA engine; AI‑driven reinforcement learning can trim electricity use by **≈ 15 %** and improve net‑present‑value by **≥ 5 %** while keeping GWP ≤ 0.8 kg CO₂‑eq kg⁻¹ product.

**B. Divergent Claims (statement ↔ counter‑statement)**

  **Counter‑statement:** “When fouling‑induced electricity spikes of **+30 %** occur, the same high‑load operation can become uneconomic unless anti‑fouling measures are applied.”

  **Counter‑statement:** “In highly variable wastewater streams, the same AI controller may increase O&M (cleaning) costs by **≈ 20 %** due to more frequent fouling‑mitigation cycles.”

  **Counter‑statement:** “The loop introduces a 0.5 kWh kg⁻¹ cleaning energy penalty; if electricity is grid‑sourced with high carbon intensity, overall GWP can rise by **≈ 5 %**.”
