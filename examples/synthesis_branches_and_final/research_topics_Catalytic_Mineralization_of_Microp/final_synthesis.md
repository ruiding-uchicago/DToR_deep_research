2) Synthesized Findings: Convergent Themes, Evidence, and Performance Benchmarks

Core concepts that recur across the branches include: (a) ROS-rich catalyst architectures that sustain high fluxes of hydroxyl radicals and related ROS, (b) mineralization and depolymerization metrics that approach or exceed 85–90% TOC removal with tight carbon-balance accounting, (c) consideration of real-water matrices (seawater, high salinity, turbidity, natural organic matter) and the need for durable, regenerable catalysts, and (d) integration of real-time analytics and feedback control to optimize energy use and maintain mineralization while mitigating by-products.

Representative findings and evidence across the three perspectives:

- Catalyst‑centric ROS generation and polymer degradation
  - ROS‑rich heterojunctions are repeatedly highlighted as effective workhorses. Examples include defect‑engineered TiO2–g‑C3N4, Fe‑doped BiOx/BiOI, Cu2O/TiO2, and Co3O4/BOC, which consistently deliver fluxes of 1–2 × 10−6 M s−1 for ROS such as •OH, O2•−, and 1O2. Reported outcomes include >85% PET mineralization and >80% backbone scission of polyolefins within 48–72 h under simulated solar illumination.
  - Core–shell and MOF-derived architectures amplify ROS generation and durability. Au‑Zr‑MOF shells (5 wt% Au) raise 1O2 quantum yield to ΦΔ ≈ 0.68 (about 2.3× higher than bare MOF). A CdS/CeO2 core coupled system with such shells can maintain >90% structural integrity after 48 h in seawater (Au leaching <0.02 mg L−1).
  - Conductive polymer shells (PEDOT:PSS) serve as metal-free, recharge-stable interfaces that retain a reservoir of H2O2 (∼100 mg L−1) and convert H2O2 to •OH with a turnover frequency (TOF) ≈ 0.8 s−1, enabling >85% degradation of PET and PE microplastics in under 6 h.
  - Real-time ROS analytics enable closed-loop control. PtOEP phosphorescence probes (LOD 0.05 µM) together with RRDE detection of SO4•− (1.2 × 10−6 M s−1) enable feedback-regulated irradiation (~150 mW cm−2) that cuts energy use by ~20% without sacrificing mineralization.
  - Enzymatic safety nets: Immobilised LCC hydrolase (78% activity after 30 days) in magnetic biochar carriers, integrated with HEMA‑methacrylate gels, complement ROS-based pathways by accelerating PET glycolysis to BHET (+12% conversion) while showing negligible aquatic cytotoxicity.

- Realistic matrices, durability, and true-cleanup benchmarks
  - Durability under realistic matrices is reported: core–shell particles retain >90% activity after 48 h in seawater; Cu2+ leaching from TiO2–Cu–LDH remains <0.12 mg L−1 after 2 weeks; magnetic recovery cycles show <2% ROS loss per 10-run batch, suggesting field-appropriate stability.
  - Toxicology and regulatory benchmarks are integral to the “true cleanup” definition. The Defining ‘True Cleanup’ branch emphasizes three pillars: TOC removal (≥85–90%, with ≥95% carbon balance accounting for all fingerprinted products), residual toxicity (≤10% of control EC50 or Tier‑2 benchmark), and metal leaching (Fe ≤0.2 mg L−1, Cu ≤2 mg L−1, Ce ≤0.1 mg L−1, all below drinking-water limits). Mineralization timescales in LED/photothermal AOPs and Fe/MOF or Fe‑PILC catalysts are typically 30–120 minutes with good catalyst reuse (≥10 cycles, ≤3% activity loss). Life-cycle analyses indicate low global warming potential (GWP) for solar/LED-driven photothermal-Fenton systems (≤0.15 kg CO2-eq per m3; about 70% lower than incineration) and limited eutrophication potential (≤0.008 kg PO4-eq per m3).

- Micromotor architectures for in‑situ ROS delivery
  - Micromotor designs combining UV-active Janus catalysts (e.g., Pt/Ag3VO4, Au/TiO2, TiO2–Au) with an on-board MoS2 photodetector and a high-frequency magnetic/DEP actuation system deliver ROS efficiently in turbid water (≥200 NTU) with swimming speeds in the 200–300 µm s−1 range, and ROS fluxes >0.8 µmol L−1 min−1 mg−1 •OH.
  - Ultrathin MO-type shells on magnetic helices preserve >80% of native propulsion (~200 µm s−1) while providing continuous H2O2 production (~1.2 µmol min−1 cm−2), enabling fuel-free operation in real seawater (35 ppt).
  - Swarm coordination and self-cleaning photocatalytic shells allow reductions in bulk catalyst loading (≈30%) while achieving >90% microplastic mineralization within 30 minutes.
  - Real-time ROS sensing via embedded amperometric microelectrodes (LOD ≈ 15 nM) or fluorescence probes (CM-H2DCFDA LOD ≈ 2.36 nM) enables closed-loop PID control that maintains target •OH flux with energy savings (~22%), and AI‑assisted optical delivery (NATOF tapered fibers) expands illumination depth in turbid water.
  - Long-term fouling control and reduced leaching achieved with graphene–QD MOF coatings and protective shells reduce Cu leaching to ≤0.1 mg L−1 and Ru leaching to ≤0.07 mg L−1 over multi-cycle operation, maintaining >90% catalytic activity after ≥48 h in saline and alkaline conditions.
  - Integrated optical delivery and magnetic steering extend functional illumination under cloudy conditions, maintaining ROS generation even when natural light is limited.

3) Contradiction Analysis & Resolution

The integrated landscape reveals several notable tensions between claims and counterclaims across perspectives. Where possible, these tensions are reconciled by clarifying scope, boundary conditions, and the need for standardization:

- Scale‑up and throughput versus lab‑scale performance
  - Claim: Photothermal-Fenton hybrids and related ROS generators can achieve near‑complete mineralization rapidly in lab settings; claims of pilot deployment rely on continuous-flow synthesis and reactor design.
  - Counterclaim: Kilogram‑per‑hour throughput is not demonstrated, and no comprehensive cost analysis exists. Resolution: The reality is that many high‑performing systems demonstrate impressive lab‑scale performance (e.g., ≥95% mineralization within 30–120 min) but lack validated scales and cost models. The field recognizes the necessity of standardizing processes (MAP, BMD) and reporting pilot‑scale metrics to bridge this gap. The debate persists until scale‑up data, energy footprints, and capital expenditures are reported concurrently with performance.

- Stability of catalysts and metals in real waters
  - Claim: Core–shell MOFs and metal-containing catalysts exhibit minimal leaching in seawater over defined periods (e.g., Au leaching <0.02 mg L−1 after 48 h). 
  - Counterclaim: In some contexts, trace metal release (Fe, Cu, Ru) can exceed thresholds under certain pH or chloride conditions or during long-term operation.
  - Resolution: The discrepant observations likely reflect matrix variability (pH, salinity, NOM), kinetic aging, and different testing durations. The consensus is that long-term, field‑relevant testing across diverse water chemistries is essential, with explicit reporting of leaching as a function of time, pH, and ionic strength.

- Enzymatic stability under illumination
  - Claim: Enzyme-embedded systems (e.g., PETase with immobilization in HEMA gels) exhibit robust activity and negligible cytotoxicity over 30 days.
  - Counterclaim: Enzyme deactivation under UV illumination can drop activity by >30% after a short irradiation window, challenging concurrent high-yield depolymerization and mineralization.
  - Resolution: These perspectives point to a trade‑off: combining enzymes with photocatalysis offers synergy but requires strategies to mitigate photo‑denaturation (e.g., shielding wavelengths, spatial separation, or protective matrices). The field accepts that enzyme stability is context‑dependent and that robust, real-world performance will rely on protective design and lifecycle testing.

- Energy intensity versus throughput in micromotors
  - Claim: AI‑assisted micromotors can deliver ROS efficiently with energy savings via closed-loop control and optimized illumination.
  - Counterclaim: Some studies show higher energy demands with alternative activation modalities (e.g., microwave-assisted Fenton) while achieving faster kinetics.
  - Resolution: The micromotor literature indicates a trade-off between energy input and reaction rate, with closed-loop control and optimized illumination reducing energy consumption by roughly 20% in reported systems. Real‑world deployment will require a nuanced optimization algorithm that balances energy, ROS delivery, and polymer mineralization, especially in complex matrices.

- Real-time sensing latency and control loop efficacy
  - Claim: Real-time ROS sensing and PID control can guarantee immediate or near-immediate toxicity control.
  - Counterclaim: Reported latencies of up to 15 minutes in some setups may allow toxic intermediates to accumulate.
  - Resolution: The contradiction arises from differences in sensor technologies and communication pathways. The field acknowledges that truly immediate control requires minimal sensing-to-action latency, with ongoing development of faster transduction, data processing, and actuation loops to tighten response times.

Overall, these contradictions reflect the evolving state of play: impressive laboratory demonstrations are contrasted with the complexities of real-world matrices, scale-up constraints, economic feasibility, and regulatory toxicity benchmarks. The resolutions proposed—standardized protocols (MAP, BMD), explicit reporting of leaching and toxicity across cycles and matrices, and scalable, integrated system designs—provide a clear pathway toward reconciling the branches.

5) Comprehensive Conclusion

Integrated synthesis of the three perspectives reveals a converging vision: verifiable depolymerization and mineralization of common microplastics in real waters is attainable with carefully engineered ROS-generating photocatalysts and, in some designs, complementary micromotor architectures. The strongest, most credible demonstrations combine (i) high ROS fluxes enabling rapid depolymerization/mineralization, (ii) structural durability and minimal leaching in realistic matrices, (iii) explicit toxicology and mass-balance validation to establish “true cleanup,” and (iv) standardized reporting to facilitate cross‑study comparisons. Across the branches, the following themes emerge as pivotal for progress toward practical deployment:

- Catalyst architectures that maximize ROS production while preserving stability in real waters (e.g., defect‑engineered TiO2–g‑C3N4; Fe‑doped BiOx/BiOI; Cu2O/TiO2; Co3O4/BOC; Au‑decorated MOFs; PEDOT:PSS shells) consistently yield meaningful mineralization fractions, with PET mineralization often exceeding 80–90% under realistic illumination, and polyolefin backbone cleavage approaching similar orders of magnitude in optimized systems.

- Core‑shell MOF and plasmonic composite designs show promise for high 1O2 quantum yields and robust performance in challenging matrices (seawater exposure tests, leaching control). The practical advantage of these architectures is counterbalanced by real‑world scale‑up questions (throughput, cost, stability under diverse water chemistries).

- “True cleanup” requires more than mineralization efficiency; it demands comprehensive toxicology benchmarking and life-cycle stewardship. The proposed pillars—TOC removal (85–90% plus carbon balance ≥95%), residual toxicity below defined ecotoxicology thresholds, and metal leaching well below drinking-water limits—provide a credible framework for assessing progress beyond lab-scale metrics.

- Micromotor architectures introduce a compelling route to localized ROS delivery in complex environments, especially with closed-loop sensing and control. The combination of Janus catalysts, on-board sensing, swarm strategies, and energy‑optimized illumination can deliver targeted mineralization while mitigating energy burdens. However, field deployment must address long‑term fouling, stability, and the integration of sensing–actuation cycles in real-world water chemistries.

- A clear path forward is to couple the catalytic advances with standardized reporting protocols (MAP, BMD), robust LCA, and long‑term field testing across water types (fresh, brackish, seawater with varied NOM). Such a strategy would produce results that not only demonstrate high mineralization fractions but also satisfy environmental safety, economic viability, and regulatory expectations for true cleanup.

6) Final Thoughts

The integrated view supports a cautious optimism: through judicious combinations of ROS‑generating photocatalysts, robust downstream mineralization assessment, and innovative micromotor architectures with closed‑loop control, verifiable depolymerization/mineralization of PET, PE, and PP in real waters is increasingly plausible. The strongest candidates combine high ROS flux with matrix durability, explicit mineralization and carbon-balance metrics, and toxicity/leaching benchmarks that satisfy the “true cleanup” definition. Realizing practical, scalable systems will demand:

- Standardized reporting and regulatory‑readiness: consistent adoption of MAP and BMD toxicology modeling, and parallel LCA metrics to quantify environmental footprints.

- Comprehensive, real‑world testing: long‑term durability studies across water chemistries (fresh, brackish, seawater), biofouling, and variable pH/ NOM exposure; extended cycle lifetimes beyond 20–30 cycles.

- Scale‑up and economics: validated pilot‑ to municipal‑scale processes with transparent CAPEX/OPEX, energy balance, and cost per m3 treated.

- Integrated device platforms: continued development of closed‑loop sensing–actuation for micromotors, robust protective coatings to minimize metal leaching in field conditions, and streamlined manufacturing routes for MOF/heterojunction architectures.

Ultimately, the synergy between catalyst design, toxicological benchmarking, and active micro/micromotor systems will be the cornerstone of achieving verifiable, safe, and scalable “true cleanup” of microplastics in real-world waters.
