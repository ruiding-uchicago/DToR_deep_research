# Branch Synthesis: Vertical vs. Lateral Device Architectures for Maximizing Mobility and Responsivity\n## Branch ID: a43043e85842459c\n## Depth: 3\n\n**A. Consolidated Insights (≈ 6 bullets)**  

- **Vertical VS₂/MoS₂ stacks** deliver ultra‑short (2–5 nm) channels that give **mobility > 10 cm² V⁻¹ s⁻¹**, **responsivity ≈ 30 A W⁻¹**, **EQE ≈ 25 %**, **NEP ≈ 10⁻¹² W Hz⁻¹ᐟ²**, and **10–70 ns** response, making them the fastest low‑noise transducers in the mixed‑dimensional family.  
- **Lateral mixed‑dimensional devices** (e.g., ReS₂/MoS₂ hybrids, MoS₂‑QD photogating) achieve **much higher gain** (R ≈ 5 × 10² A W⁻¹, EQE > 1 000 %) but suffer **slow response (≥ 10 µs)** and **higher NEP (10⁻⁹ W Hz⁻¹ᐟ²)** because carrier transport is limited by long in‑plane paths and trap‑dominated 1/f noise.  
- **Mobility gap**: lateral WSe₂‑Te channels reach **≈ 11 cm² V⁻¹ s⁻¹**, roughly **3×** the mobility of pure vertical VS₂/MoS₂ stacks (≈ 3.6 cm² V⁻¹ s⁻¹) when the latter are not engineered for high‑κ gating or contact optimization.  
- **Noise origin** diverges: vertical devices are **Δμ‑fluctuation limited** (mobility‑fluctuation noise) with a weak temperature dependence, whereas lateral devices are **Δn‑fluctuation (trap‑dominated) limited**, showing a strong temperature‑driven reduction of Hooge’s α_H.  
- **Field‑shaping & strain engineering** (multi‑ring concentric gates, nano‑folds, biaxial tensile strain) can boost vertical responsivity by **≈ 20–40 %** and raise breakdown voltage by **≈ 30 %**, while lateral devices gain ≈ 40 % photocurrent when the channel is aligned with Te‑rich rings.  
- **Hybrid proposals** (lateral high‑mobility WSe₂‑Te core + vertical VS₂/MoS₂ contacts, edge‑localized MoS₂‑QD photogating) aim to combine the **high mobility of lateral channels** with the **fast, low‑noise vertical field‑enhancement**, targeting **μ ≈ 9–10 cm² V⁻¹ s⁻¹**, **BV + 30 %**, and **1/f noise ↓ ≈ 40 %** versus pure vertical stacks.

**B. Divergent Claims (statement ↔ counter‑statement)**  

- **Claim:** “Vertical VS₂/MoS₂ devices exhibit higher mobility than lateral WSe₂‑Te channels.”  
  **Counter‑claim:** “Lateral WSe₂‑Te channels achieve μ ≈ 11 cm² V⁻¹ s⁻¹, which is ≈ 3× the mobility of the VS₂/MoS₂ vertical stack (≈ 3.6 cm² V⁻¹ s⁻¹).”  

- **Claim:** “Lateral mixed‑dimensional phototransistors provide the best responsivity because of strong photogating.”  
  **Counter‑claim:** “Vertical VS₂/MoS₂ stacks, when combined with edge‑localized MoS₂‑QD layers, reach EQE ≈ 30 % and maintain sub‑100 ns response, outperforming lateral devices in speed‑limited applications.”  

- **Claim:** “1/f noise in vertical devices is negligible compared with lateral devices.”  
  **Counter‑claim:** “Both vertical and lateral devices are dominated by Δμ‑fluctuation noise; the absolute PSD values differ only by a factor of ~10, and vertical devices still show measurable 1/f noise that scales with active volume.”  

- **Claim:** “Multi‑ring concentric gates universally improve breakdown voltage and reduce noise for any 2‑D channel.”  
  **Counter‑claim:** “The BV uplift (~30 %) and noise reduction (~20 %) have only been demonstrated in Si LDMOS simulations; experimental verification on atomically thin WSe₂ or MoS₂ is still lacking.”  

- **Claim:** “Strain‑induced band‑gap funnels always increase photocurrent by ~40 %."  
  **Counter‑claim:** “The 40 % boost was observed for 1.6 % biaxial strain in WS₂ nano‑folds; other TMDs and strain magnitudes may not follow the same trend, and excessive strain can introduce additional traps that raise 1/f noise.”

**C. Notable Gaps (≈ 3 bullets)**  

- **Unified mobility‑/noise matrix:** No study reports simultaneous μ and 1/f‑noise (α_H or PSD) for lateral, vertical, and hybrid devices, preventing a quantitative trade‑off analysis.  
- **Experimental validation of multi‑ring field‑shaping on 2‑D channels:** The predicted 30 % BV uplift and 20 % peak‑field reduction remain simulation‑only; real devices have not yet demonstrated these gains.  
- **Long‑term reliability of hybrid vertical‑lateral stacks:** Fatigue, bias‑stress, and temperature cycling effects on contact resistance, trap density, and NEP for proposed hybrid architectures have not been measured.
